About the Book

A Journalist's Memoir & Urgent Plea

At least 55 million Americans now have little to no access to local news in their communities — and that number grows every day. Rick Thames, who spent nearly four decades in journalism, including 13 years as Executive Editor and Vice President for News at The Charlotte Observer, has written a book that should matter to everyone who cares about democracy, community, and truth.

Why Local News weaves together memoir, reporting, and advocacy — drawing on Thames' career from uncovering military parachute failures at Fort Bragg to leading one of the South's great newspapers — to make the case for why local journalism is worth fighting to save.

Published by McFarland Books, Why Local News also profiles the journalists and outlets across the country still doing the work, and offers a vision for how communities can come together to rescue this endangered but essential institution.

The Author

About Rick Thames

Rick Thames spent nearly four decades in American journalism, building a career that took him from community papers to the top editorial post at one of the South's most respected daily newspapers.

He served as Executive Editor and Vice President for News at The Charlotte Observer from 2004 to 2017, where he led the paper through an era of digital transformation, Pulitzer Prize–recognized work, and relentless community journalism.

Earlier in his career, Thames broke major stories — including an investigation into parachute equipment failures at Fort Bragg that resulted in the deaths of seven soldiers — stories that demonstrated the unique power of local journalists to hold institutions accountable.

Today, Rick teaches journalism at Queens University of Charlotte and continues to advocate for the future of local news. Why Local News is his first book.
